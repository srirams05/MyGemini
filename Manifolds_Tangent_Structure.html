Introduction

Our discussion has covered the following key objects in differential geometry, focusing on smooth manifolds:

  1. Smooth Manifolds: The fundamental objects, locally resembling Euclidean space but possessing a globally smooth structure.

  2. Charts and Atlases: Charts provide local coordinate systems on a manifold, mapping open subsets of the manifold to open subsets of \(\mathbb{R}^n\). An atlas is a collection of charts that cover the entire manifold and whose transition maps are smooth, ensuring the smooth structure.

  3. Tangent Space (\(T_pM\)): The vector space at each point \(p\) on a manifold \(M\), representing the space of directional derivatives or equivalence classes of curves through \(p\).

  4. Cotangent Space (\(T_p^*M\)): The dual space of the tangent space, consisting of linear functionals (covectors or one-forms) acting on tangent vectors.

  5. Tangent Bundle (\(TM\)): The smooth manifold formed by the union of all tangent spaces, providing a smooth way to work with tangent vectors across the entire manifold. It’s a vector bundle.

  6. Cotangent Bundle (\(T^*M\)): The smooth manifold formed by the union of all cotangent spaces, analogous to the tangent bundle but using covectors. It’s a vector bundle.

  7. Vector Fields (\(\mathfrak{X}(M)\)): Smooth sections of the tangent bundle, assigning a tangent vector to each point of \(M\). They form a module over \(C^\infty(M)\) and a Lie algebra.

  8. Lie Derivative (\(\mathcal{L}_X\)): The directional derivative along a vector field \(X\), mapping smooth functions to smooth functions.

  9. Lie Bracket (\([X, Y]\)): A bilinear, antisymmetric operation on vector fields, satisfying the Jacobi identity. It measures the “failure of commutativity” of flows and defines the Lie algebra structure on \(\mathfrak{X}(M)\).

  10. Tangent Map (\(T_pf\)): For a smooth map \(f: M \to N\), the tangent map at point \(p \in M\) is a linear map from \(T_pM\) to \(T_{f(p)}N\). It maps equivalence classes of curves to equivalence classes of curves and represents the “best linear approximation” to \(f\) at \(p\).

  11. Differential of a Map (\(df_p\)): For a smooth map \(f: M \to N\), the differential at point \(p \in M\) is the linear map from \(T_pM\) to \(T_{f(p)}N\) equivalent to the tangent map. It describes the action of \(f\) on tangent vectors at \(p\).

  12. Pushforward (\(f_*X\)) and Pullback (\(f^*Y\)): Operations that map vector fields between manifolds under a diffeomorphism \(f\), using the differential of \(f\) and its inverse.

  13. Diffeomorphisms: Smooth, invertible maps between manifolds, which are crucial for relating geometric structures on different manifolds.

These objects and their interrelationships form a significant part of the foundation of differential geometry. Charts and atlases are fundamental for constructing the smooth structure itself. The tangent map and differential are crucial for understanding how smooth maps affect the tangent spaces.

Tangent Space

My question is about smooth manifolds. Let \(M\) be a smooth manifold. Describe the two ways to characterize the tangent space at a point \(p \in M\) * using equivalence class of curves tangent at \(p\) * as the set of derivations at \(p\) acting on \(f \in C^\infty(M)\)## Heading

Let \(M\) be a smooth \(n\)-dimensional manifold. Let’s describe the two ways to characterize the tangent space \(T_pM\) at a point \(p \in M\):

1. Equivalence Classes of Curves Tangent at \(p\)

  • Curves: Consider smooth curves \(\gamma_1\) and \(\gamma_2\) passing through \(p \in M\). We can represent these curves as maps \(\gamma_i: (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M\), where \(\epsilon > 0\), such that \(\gamma_i(0) = p\) for \(i = 1, 2\).

  • Tangent Vectors as Velocities: The velocity vector of a curve \(\gamma\) at \(p\) is given by the derivative of its coordinate representation in a chart. If \((U, \varphi)\) is a chart around \(p\), with \(\varphi(p) = 0\), then the velocity vector is \(\gamma'(0) = \frac{d}{dt}(\varphi \circ \gamma)|_{t=0} \in \mathbb{R}^n\). This velocity vector gives the instantaneous direction and speed of the curve at \(p\).

  • Equivalence Relation: Two curves \(\gamma_1\) and \(\gamma_2\) are considered equivalent at \(p\) if they have the same velocity vector in any chart around \(p\). This is well-defined because a change of charts induces a linear transformation on the velocity vectors, preserving the notion of “same velocity” (the Jacobian matrix of the coordinate transformation acts on the velocity vectors).

  • Tangent Space: The tangent space \(T_pM\) is defined as the set of equivalence classes of curves passing through \(p\), where equivalence is defined as above. Each equivalence class represents a tangent vector at \(p\). The velocity vector in a particular chart provides coordinates for this tangent vector.

2. Derivations at \(p\)

  • Smooth Functions: Let \(C^\infty(M)\) denote the set of all smooth real-valued functions on \(M\).

  • Derivations: A derivation at \(p\) is a linear map \(X_p: C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}\) satisfying the Leibniz rule:

    \(X_p(fg) = f(p)X_p(g) + g(p)X_p(f)\) for all \(f, g \in C^\infty(M)\).

    This rule captures the essence of the directional derivative: it’s linear and satisfies the product rule.

  • Tangent Space as Derivations: The tangent space \(T_pM\) is defined as the set of all derivations at \(p\). Each derivation represents a tangent vector.

  • Connection between the two definitions: Given a curve \(\gamma\) through \(p\), its velocity vector defines a derivation at \(p\):

    \(X_\gamma(f) = \frac{d}{dt}(f \circ \gamma)|_{t=0}\)

This maps the velocity vector of the curve to a derivation; it acts on a smooth function \(f\) by taking its directional derivative along the curve at \(p\). Conversely, given a derivation \(X_p\), one can show it corresponds to the velocity vector of some curve passing through \(p\).

In essence, both definitions characterize the tangent space as a vector space of dimension \(n\) (equal to the dimension of the manifold), providing different but equivalent perspectives on the concept of a tangent vector at a point on a manifold. The first uses geometric intuition of curves, and the second uses an algebraic approach focusing on the action of tangent vectors on functions. They are connected through the directional derivative.

Let’s elaborate on the connection between a derivation at a point \(p\) on a manifold \(M\) and a directional derivative.

Recall the definition: A derivation at \(p\), denoted \(X_p\), is a linear map \(X_p: C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}\) satisfying the Leibniz rule:

\(X_p(fg) = f(p)X_p(g) + g(p)X_p(f)\) for all \(f, g \in C^\infty(M)\).

Now, consider a directional derivative. In \(\mathbb{R}^n\), the directional derivative of a function \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\) in the direction of a vector \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) at a point \(\mathbf{x}\) is given by:

\(D_{\mathbf{v}}f(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{x}) v_i\)

This is a linear operator on functions (linearity in \(f\) and \(\mathbf{v}\)) and satisfies the Leibniz rule:

\(D_{\mathbf{v}}(fg)(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla(fg)(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = (f(\mathbf{x})\nabla g(\mathbf{x}) + g(\mathbf{x})\nabla f(\mathbf{x})) \cdot \mathbf{v} = f(\mathbf{x})D_{\mathbf{v}}g(\mathbf{x}) + g(\mathbf{x})D_{\mathbf{v}}f(\mathbf{x})\)

The key similarities between a derivation \(X_p\) and a directional derivative \(D_{\mathbf{v}}\) are:

  1. Linearity: Both are linear maps acting on functions. They both satisfy \(X_p(\alpha f + \beta g) = \alpha X_p(f) + \beta X_p(g)\) and similarly for \(D_v\).

  2. Leibniz Rule: Both satisfy the product rule (Leibniz rule). This is crucial because it means they act like differentiation; they “distribute” across products in a way that mimics the derivative’s behavior.

  3. Local nature: Both are defined at a specific point. The directional derivative \(D_{\mathbf{v}}f(\mathbf{x})\) is calculated at the point \(\mathbf{x}\), and the derivation \(X_p\) acts on functions evaluated at \(p\).

The difference is that a directional derivative is defined in Euclidean space using partial derivatives, which are not directly available on a general manifold. A derivation provides an abstraction of the directional derivative that works on manifolds without needing explicit coordinates or partial derivatives. A derivation captures the essence of “directional change of a function at a point” in a coordinate-free manner. We then show that derivations are in one-to-one correspondence with tangent vectors defined via curves, giving us a concrete connection. The chart mapping gives us the link between the abstract derivation and the coordinate representation of the directional derivative.

\(X_p = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i\) for some \(a_i \in \mathbb{R}\).

Therefore, instead of viewing the partial derivative operator as directly acting on \(f\), we use the partial derivatives within a chart to construct the derivations which are then the fundamental objects capturing directional derivatives on the manifold. The derivations are chart-independent in that they represent a tangent vector irrespective of the choice of local coordinates, even though they are initially defined using a coordinate chart.

Tangent Bundle

Next Question Now that the tangent space is defined, I want to discuss the tangent bundle. Each point \(p \in M\) has an associated tangent space \(T_pM\). The collection of these tangent spaces can be organized into a tangent bundle. I can view this collection as a set but what more structure can be given to it.

You’re right, the collection of tangent spaces \(\{T_pM\}_{p \in M}\) can be organized into a structure called the tangent bundle, denoted \(TM\). Viewing it simply as a set is insufficient; it needs a lot more structure to be useful in differential geometry. Here’s how we equip it with that structure:

  1. Set: The underlying set of the tangent bundle is indeed the disjoint union of all tangent spaces:

    \(TM = \bigcup_{p \in M} T_pM\)

  2. Projection Map: There’s a natural projection map \(\pi: TM \to M\) which sends each tangent vector \(v \in T_pM\) to the point \(p\) to which it’s tangent: \(\pi(v) = p\). This map is crucial because it connects the tangent vectors back to their base points on the manifold.

  3. Topology: We equip \(TM\) with a topology. Several topologies are possible, but the most common and useful is the topology induced by the charts of \(M\). Given a chart \((U, \varphi)\) on \(M\), we construct a corresponding chart \((\pi^{-1}(U), \tilde{\varphi})\) on \(TM\) as follows: If \(v \in T_pM\) for some \(p \in U\), then \(v\) can be represented as a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) (using the chart). The map \(\tilde{\varphi}\) sends \(v\) to \((\varphi(p), v_{\mathbb{R}^n})\), where \(v_{\mathbb{R}^n}\) is the coordinate representation of \(v\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). This essentially gives each tangent space local coordinates. This construction makes \(\pi\) a continuous map.

  4. Smooth Manifold Structure: The most important addition is giving \(TM\) the structure of a smooth manifold. The charts \((\pi^{-1}(U), \tilde{\varphi})\) constructed above, with \(\tilde{\varphi}\) mapping to \(\mathbb{R}^{2n}\) (n coordinates for the base point and n for the tangent vector), precisely define this smooth structure. This makes the tangent bundle itself a smooth manifold of dimension 2n, if M is of dimension n.

  5. Smooth Vector Bundle Structure: The tangent bundle is more specifically a smooth vector bundle. This adds extra structure beyond just being a manifold:

    • Vector Space Structure on Fibers: For each \(p \in M\), the fiber \(\pi^{-1}(p) = T_pM\) is a vector space (the tangent space at \(p\)). The vector space operations (addition and scalar multiplication) are smoothly defined.
    • Local Trivialization: There exist local trivializations. This means that for each open set \(U\) in \(M\), there is a diffeomorphism between \(\pi^{-1}(U)\) and \(U \times \mathbb{R}^n\) that preserves the vector space structure in the fibers. Essentially, locally, the tangent bundle looks like a Cartesian product of the manifold and its tangent space.

In summary, the tangent bundle is much more than just a set; it’s a smooth manifold (with twice the dimension of the original manifold) equipped with a smooth projection map, a vector bundle structure, and local coordinate charts. This rich structure allows for powerful tools from differential geometry to be applied and used to analyze vector fields, differential forms, and other fundamental objects on manifolds.

Tangent Space - Basis of (Derivation)

New Question Going back to the tangent space. How do you show that \(\partial / \partial x_i\) is a basis for the tangent space given a specific chart

To show that \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\) forms a basis for the tangent space \(T_pM\) at a point \(p\) given a specific chart \((U, \varphi)\), we need to connect the abstract definition of a tangent vector (as a derivation or equivalence class of curves) to its coordinate representation using the chart.

Let’s assume that \((U, \varphi)\) is a chart around \(p\), and \(\varphi: U \subset M \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is a diffeomorphism onto its image. We’ll use the derivation approach to define the tangent vectors.

  1. Derivations as Tangent Vectors: Recall that a tangent vector at \(p\) can be defined as a derivation \(X_p: C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}\), which is a linear map satisfying the Leibniz rule:

    \(X_p(fg) = f(p)X_p(g) + g(p)X_p(f)\) for all \(f, g \in C^\infty(M)\).

  2. Constructing the Basis Derivations: We define \(n\) derivations at \(p\) using the chart \((U, \varphi)\):

    \(\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p(f) = \frac{\partial(f \circ \varphi^{-1})}{\partial x_i}(\varphi(p))\) for \(i = 1, \dots, n\)

    Here, \(f \circ \varphi^{-1}\) is the expression of \(f\) in local coordinates given by the chart. This formula takes the partial derivative of the coordinate representation of \(f\) with respect to the \(i\)-th coordinate and evaluates it at the point \(\varphi(p)\). It’s important to note that this derivation is well-defined, independent of the choice of coordinate representation.

  3. Linear Independence: The derivations \(\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p\) are linearly independent. To see this, suppose that:

    \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p = 0\)

    This means that for any smooth function \(f\), we have:

    \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p(f) = 0\)

    If we let \(f = x_j\) (the \(j\)-th coordinate function), then \(\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p(x_j) = \delta_{ij}\) (the Kronecker delta). Therefore, the above equation implies \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i \delta_{ij} = a_j = 0\) for all \(j = 1, \dots, n\). This proves linear independence.

  4. Spanning the Tangent Space: Any derivation \(X_p\) at \(p\) can be written as a linear combination of the \(\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p\). This is because we can show that any smooth function can be expanded using Taylor expansion around \(\varphi(p)\) in the local coordinates given by the chart, and it becomes evident that the \(\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p\) generate all possible directional derivatives at \(p\).

Therefore, because the \(\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\right)_p\) are linearly independent and span the tangent space \(T_pM\), they form a basis for \(T_pM\). This basis is coordinate-dependent; different charts give different bases.

    \(T_p^*M = \text{Hom}(T_pM, \mathbb{R})\)

    An element \(\omega_p \in T_p^*M\) is a linear map \(\omega_p: T_pM \to \mathbb{R}\). Such a map is called a covector or a one-form at \(p\).

  3. Dimension: Since \(T_pM\) has dimension \(n\), its dual space \(T_p^*M\) also has dimension \(n\). If \(\{e_1, \dots, e_n\}\) is a basis for \(T_pM\), then the dual basis \(\{\theta^1, \dots, \theta^n\}\) for \(T_p^*M\) is defined by:

    \(\theta^i(e_j) = \delta^i_j\)

    where \(\delta^i_j\) is the Kronecker delta (1 if \(i=j\), 0 otherwise). Any covector \(\omega_p\) can be uniquely written as a linear combination of the basis covectors:

    \(\omega_p = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \theta^i\) for some \(a_i \in \mathbb{R}\).

  4. Differential of a Function: A particularly important way to construct covectors is using the differential of a smooth function. If \(f \in C^\infty(M)\), then its differential at \(p\), denoted \(df_p\), is a covector:

    \(df_p: T_pM \to \mathbb{R}\) \(df_p(v_p) = v_p(f)\)

    where \(v_p \in T_pM\) and \(v_p(f)\) is the directional derivative of \(f\) in the direction of \(v_p\).

In summary, the cotangent space is built by taking the dual of the tangent space. It’s a vector space of the same dimension as the tangent space, and its elements (covectors) are linear maps that act on tangent vectors. The differential of a function provides a natural way to construct covectors. The cotangent space, together with the tangent space, forms the foundation for the study of differential forms and other important objects in differential geometry.

where \(X^i(p)\) are the components of \(X\) in the coordinate basis at \(p\), and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x^i}(p)\) are the partial derivatives of \(f\) with respect to the coordinate functions at \(p\).

Lie Bracket

New Question What is a Lie bracket

Given two vector fields \(X, Y \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\) on a smooth manifold \(M\), their Lie bracket, denoted \([X, Y]\), is another vector field on \(M\). There are several ways to define it:

1. As a Derivation: The Lie bracket \([X, Y]\) is uniquely defined as the derivation that satisfies:

\([X, Y](f) = X(Y(f)) - Y(X(f))\) for all \(f \in C^\infty(M)\)

Here, \(X(f)\) and \(Y(f)\) denote the Lie derivatives of \(f\) with respect to \(X\) and \(Y\) respectively (or directional derivatives along \(X\) and \(Y\)). The right-hand side shows that the Lie bracket measures the difference between the two directional derivatives applied in sequence. The fact that this produces a derivation defines the vector field \([X,Y]\) uniquely.

2. Local Coordinate Expression: Let \((U, \varphi)\) be a chart on \(M\) with coordinates \((x^1, \dots, x^n)\). If we express the vector fields locally as:

\(X = \sum_{i=1}^n X^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\) \(Y = \sum_{i=1}^n Y^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\)

Then their Lie bracket is given by:

\([X, Y] = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \left( X^j \frac{\partial Y^i}{\partial x^j} - Y^j \frac{\partial X^i}{\partial x^j} \right) \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\)

This formula is obtained by applying the definition of \([X,Y]\) as a derivation to the coordinate functions \(x^i\) and utilizing the product rule and properties of partial derivatives.

3. Geometric Interpretation: The Lie bracket \([X, Y]\) measures the “failure of commutativity” of the flows generated by \(X\) and \(Y\). If you flow along \(X\) for a time \(t\) and then along \(Y\) for a time \(s\), and you do it in the reverse order, the difference in the final point is approximately proportional to \([X, Y]\) for small \(t\) and \(s\).

The Lie bracket is an antisymmetric bilinear operation:

\([X, Y] = -[Y, X]\) \([aX + bY, Z] = a[X, Z] + b[Y, Z]\) for \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\)

The Lie bracket plays a crucial role in Lie group theory, the study of Lie algebras (which is the vector space of vector fields under the Lie bracket operation), and many areas of differential geometry. It is a fundamental object that encodes geometric information about the interaction between vector fields.

Pullback and Pushforwards of vector fields

New Question Let \(f: M \to N\) be a smooth diffeomorphism. How to define pullbacks and pushforwards of vectors fields defined on \(M\) and \(N\)

Given a smooth diffeomorphism \(f: M \to N\) between smooth manifolds \(M\) and \(N\), we can define pushforwards and pullbacks for vector fields. Since \(f\) is a diffeomorphism, it has a smooth inverse \(f^{-1}: N \to M\).

1. Pushforward of a Vector Field:

The pushforward of a vector field \(X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\) on \(M\), denoted \(f_*X\), is a vector field on \(N\). It’s defined using the differential of \(f\):

For each \(p \in M\), \((f_*X)_{f(p)} = (df)_p (X_p) \in T_{f(p)}N\)

In words:

  • We take a point \(p\) in \(M\).
  • We consider the tangent vector \(X_p\) at that point.
  • We use the differential (or Jacobian) \((df)_p\), which is a linear map from \(T_pM\) to \(T_{f(p)}N\), to map \(X_p\) to a tangent vector at \(f(p)\) in \(N\).
  • We repeat for each point in \(M\). This produces a vector field on \(N\).

2. Pullback of a Vector Field:

The pullback of a vector field \(Y \in \mathfrak{X}(N)\) on \(N\), denoted \(f^*Y\), is a vector field on \(M\). It’s defined using the differential of the inverse map \(f^{-1}\):

For each \(q \in N\), \((f^*Y)_q = (d f^{-1})_q (Y_q) \in T_{f^{-1}(q)}M\)

In words:

  • We take a point \(q\) in \(N\).
  • We consider the tangent vector \(Y_q\) at that point.
  • We use the differential \((df^{-1})_q\), which is a linear map from \(T_qN\) to \(T_{f^{-1}(q)}M\), to map \(Y_q\) to a tangent vector in \(M\).
  • We repeat for each point in \(N\). This produces a vector field on \(M\).

Important Notes:

  • Diffeomorphism Requirement: The existence of a smooth inverse \(f^{-1}\) is crucial for defining the pullback. If \(f\) is only a smooth map (not a diffeomorphism), then the pullback of a vector field is not well-defined.

  • Linearity: Both pushforward and pullback are linear operations. They preserve the vector space structure of the space of vector fields.

  • Relationship to Tangent Map: The pushforward of a vector field is closely related to the tangent map \(T_p f\), which maps tangent vectors at a point in \(M\) to tangent vectors at the corresponding point in \(N\). The pushforward simply extends this pointwise action to vector fields (which are smooth assignments of tangent vectors).

These operations are essential in differential geometry for studying how diffeomorphisms affect vector fields, enabling a comparison of vector fields on different manifolds. They also play a role in various applications, such as the study of Lie group actions on manifolds.

The pushforward of a function \(f \in C^\infty(M)\), denoted \(\phi_*f\), is a function in \(C^\infty(N)\). It’s defined similarly to the pullback but using the inverse diffeomorphism \(\phi^{-1}\) (which exists because \(\phi\) is a diffeomorphism):

\((\phi_*f)(q) = f(\phi^{-1}(q))\) for all \(q \in N\)

Here, we evaluate \(f\) at the point \(\phi^{-1}(q)\) in \(M\) to get the value of \(\phi_*f\) at the point \(q\) in \(N\). Again, the smoothness of \(f\) and \(\phi^{-1}\) guarantees that \(\phi_*f\) is a smooth function.

Important Note: The pushforward and pullback of functions are linear operations. If you have two functions \(f_1\) and \(f_2\), then:

\(\phi_*(af_1 + bf_2) = a\phi_*f_1 + b\phi_*f_2\) and \(\phi^*(ag_1 + bg_2) = a\phi^*g_1 + b\phi^*g_2\) for \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\).

The pullback is particularly important because it allows us to “transfer” functions from one manifold to another via the diffeomorphism, preserving the smoothness. This is a basic but powerful technique in differential geometry. The pushforward serves as the inverse operation when a diffeomorphism is available.

Yes, there is a global version of the tangent map that maps between the entire tangent bundles \(TM\) and \(TN\), not just the tangent spaces at a single point. This global map is denoted \(Tf\) or sometimes \(f_*\).

Definition:

Given a smooth map \(f: M \to N\) between smooth manifolds \(M\) and \(N\), the global tangent map \(Tf: TM \to TN\) is defined as follows:

For each \((p, v_p) \in TM\), where \(p \in M\) and \(v_p \in T_pM\), we have:

\(Tf(p, v_p) = (f(p), T_p f(v_p))\)

In other words:

  • The base point \(p\) in \(M\) is mapped to its image \(f(p)\) in \(N\).
  • The tangent vector \(v_p \in T_pM\) is mapped to its image \(T_p f(v_p) \in T_{f(p)}N\) under the tangent map at the point \(p\).

Properties:

  • \(Tf\) is a smooth map between the tangent bundles \(TM\) and \(TN\).
  • For each \(p \in M\), the restriction of \(Tf\) to \(T_pM\) is the tangent map \(T_p f: T_p M \to T_{f(p)} N\).
  • \(Tf\) is a vector bundle morphism; it preserves the vector space structure in the fibers. This means that for any \(p\), the map \(T_p f\) is linear.

The global tangent map \(Tf\) provides a globalized view of how the map \(f\) acts on tangent vectors across the entire manifold \(M\). It’s an important tool in differential geometry and is used extensively in studying vector fields, differential forms, and other geometric objects. It is a global version of the pointwise tangent map, extending the pointwise linear maps on tangent spaces to a smooth bundle map between the tangent bundles.

Integral Curve to a vector field

Let \(X\) be a vector field on \(M\). What is the definition an Integral curve of \(X\)

Let \(X\) be a smooth vector field on a smooth manifold \(M\). An integral curve of \(X\) is a smooth curve \(\gamma: I \to M\), where \(I\) is an open interval in \(\mathbb{R}\), such that the tangent vector to the curve at each point is equal to the value of the vector field at that point.

More formally:

A smooth curve \(\gamma: I \to M\) is an integral curve of \(X\) if for all \(t \in I\):

\(\gamma'(t) = X_{\gamma(t)}\)

Here, \(\gamma'(t)\) denotes the tangent vector to the curve \(\gamma\) at the point \(\gamma(t)\), and \(X_{\gamma(t)}\) is the value of the vector field \(X\) at the point \(\gamma(t)\). In other words, the velocity vector of the curve at any time \(t\) matches the vector field’s value at the corresponding point on the manifold.

Local Coordinates:

In local coordinates \((U, \varphi)\) with \(\varphi = (x^1, \dots, x^n)\), if we express the vector field as \(X = \sum_{i=1}^n X^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\) and the curve as \(\gamma(t) = (x^1(t), \dots, x^n(t))\), the condition for \(\gamma\) to be an integral curve becomes a system of first-order ordinary differential equations (ODEs):

\(\frac{dx^i}{dt}(t) = X^i(x^1(t), \dots, x^n(t))\) for \(i = 1, \dots, n\)

Existence and Uniqueness:

The fundamental theorem on flows guarantees the existence and uniqueness of integral curves for smooth vector fields. Given a point \(p \in M\), there exists a unique maximal integral curve \(\gamma_p: I \to M\) with \(\gamma_p(0) = p\). “Maximal” means that the interval \(I\) is the largest possible open interval containing 0 for which the solution exists.

Integral curves are fundamental for understanding the behavior of vector fields. They visualize the “flow” induced by the vector field, with each integral curve tracing a path through \(M\) tangent to the vector field at every point. They are crucial for the definition of the flow of a vector field, which is a map defined from a subset of \(\mathbb{R} \times M\) to \(M\) that tells us, for any point and a time \(t\), the result of “flowing” along the vector field for the specified time. The flow is constructed from the integral curves.

Existence and Uniqueness of Integral curves - Proof idea

Can you state and prove the theorem regarding the existence and uniqueness of integral curves

The theorem you’re referring to is often called the “Fundamental Theorem on Flows” or the “Existence and Uniqueness Theorem for Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)” when applied to manifolds.

Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness of Integral Curves):

Let \(M\) be a smooth manifold and \(X\) be a smooth vector field on \(M\). For each point \(p \in M\), there exists a unique maximal integral curve \(\gamma_p: I_p \to M\) of \(X\) such that \(\gamma_p(0) = p\), where \(I_p\) is an open interval in \(\mathbb{R}\) containing 0. “Maximal” means that \(I_p\) is the largest possible open interval for which the solution exists.

Proof Outline:

The proof relies on the corresponding existence and uniqueness theorem for ODEs in Euclidean space and uses charts to transfer the result to manifolds.

  1. Local Existence and Uniqueness: Choose a chart \((U, \varphi)\) around \(p\) such that \(\varphi(p) = 0\) (we can always shift the coordinates to achieve this). In these local coordinates, the vector field \(X\) can be expressed as \(X = \sum_{i=1}^n X^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\), where \(X^i\) are smooth functions on \(U\). The condition for a curve \(\gamma(t)\) to be an integral curve becomes a system of first-order ODEs:

    \(\frac{dx^i}{dt} = X^i(x^1(t), \dots, x^n(t))\), with initial condition \(x^i(0) = 0\) (since \(\varphi(p) = 0\)).

    The standard Picard-Lindelöf theorem for ODEs in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) guarantees the existence and uniqueness of a solution to this system on some interval \((-\epsilon, \epsilon)\) for some \(\epsilon > 0\). This gives us a local integral curve in the chart.

  2. Transfer to Manifold: Using the chart map \(\varphi^{-1}\), we can map the solution in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) back to the manifold \(M\), obtaining a local integral curve \(\gamma: (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \to M\) with \(\gamma(0) = p\).

  3. Maximality and Uniqueness: To obtain a maximal integral curve, we can extend the interval of definition. Suppose \(\gamma_1\) and \(\gamma_2\) are two integral curves with \(\gamma_1(0) = \gamma_2(0) = p\). By local uniqueness within a chart, they must agree on the intersection of their intervals of definition. The maximal integral curve \(\gamma_p: I_p \to M\) is then obtained by taking the union of the intervals of definition of all integral curves through p. The uniqueness follows from the local uniqueness in charts and patching the solutions together. The maximal interval of existence, \(I_p\), is open by construction.

This proof outline provides the key ideas. The technical details involve carefully patching together local solutions and handling the transition between different charts using the smooth structure of the manifold. The Picard-Lindelöf theorem for ODEs provides the foundation for the local existence and uniqueness. Extending this to global existence and uniqueness requires some technical machinery from analysis.

Flow of a vector field

New Question: What is the formal definition of the flow of a vector field? And what is the connection with integral curves?

The flow of a vector field formalizes the idea of “flowing along” the vector field for a certain amount of time. It’s intimately connected to integral curves.

Formal Definition:

Let \(X\) be a smooth vector field on a smooth manifold \(M\). The flow of \(X\) is a smooth map:

\(\Phi: \mathcal{D} \to M\)

where \(\mathcal{D}\) is an open subset of \(\mathbb{R} \times M\) defined by \(\mathcal{D} = \{(t, p) \in \mathbb{R} \times M \,|\, t \in I_p \}\), and \(I_p\) is the maximal interval of existence for the integral curve through \(p\). The flow \(\Phi\) satisfies the following properties:

  1. \(\Phi(0, p) = p\) for all \(p \in M\). (Starting at \(t=0\) leaves the point unchanged).

  2. \(\Phi(s, \Phi(t, p)) = \Phi(s + t, p)\) whenever both sides are defined. (Flowing for time \(t\) and then for time \(s\) is the same as flowing for time \(s+t\)). This is often referred to as the group property or the flow property.

  3. For fixed \(p\), the curve \(\gamma_p(t) = \Phi(t, p)\) is the unique maximal integral curve of \(X\) starting at \(p\).

Connection with Integral Curves:

The connection between the flow and integral curves is fundamental:

  • Integral curves define the flow: The flow \(\Phi(t, p)\) is defined by “flowing” the point \(p\) along the integral curve of \(X\) that passes through \(p\) for a time \(t\). Each integral curve is a “slice” of the flow for a fixed \(p\). In other words, fixing \(p\) in the flow gives the integral curve \(\gamma_p(t) = \Phi(t,p)\).

  • The flow consists of integral curves: The flow can be thought of as a collection of all integral curves of \(X\). For each \(p \in M\), \(\Phi(\cdot, p)\) is the maximal integral curve starting at \(p\).

  • The flow determines the vector field: Conversely, given the flow \(\Phi\), the vector field \(X\) can be recovered by differentiating the flow with respect to time at \(t=0\):

    \(X_p = \frac{d}{dt}\Phi(t, p)\Big|_{t=0}\)

In essence, the flow provides a global picture of how points on the manifold move under the influence of the vector field. Each integral curve gives a local trajectory of a point, and the flow pieces them all together. The flow is a smooth map capturing the combined dynamics of all integral curves. The existence and uniqueness of integral curves imply the existence and uniqueness of the flow.

Lie Derivative of a vector field with respect to another vector field

Given \(X,Y \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\), is there a notion of a Lie derivative of \(Y\) with respect to \(X\)?

Yes, there is a notion of the Lie derivative of a vector field \(Y\) with respect to another vector field \(X\), denoted \(\mathcal{L}_X Y\). This is also known as the Lie bracket of \(X\) and \(Y\), and it’s a fundamental operation in differential geometry. It measures how the vector field \(Y\) changes along the flow of \(X\).

There are several equivalent ways to define the Lie derivative of a vector field:

1. Using flows:

Perhaps the most geometrically intuitive definition uses the flows of the vector fields. Let \(\Phi_t\) be the flow of \(X\). Then the Lie derivative of \(Y\) with respect to \(X\) at a point \(p\) is defined as:

\((\mathcal{L}_X Y)_p = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{(\Phi_{-t})_*(Y_{\Phi_t(p)}) - Y_p}{t} = \frac{d}{dt} (\Phi_{-t})_*(Y_{\Phi_t(p)})\Big|_{t=0}\)

Here, \((\Phi_{-t})_*\) is the pushforward (differential, or tangent map) of the flow \(\Phi_{-t}\). This definition captures the idea of comparing \(Y\) at a point \(p\) with the “pulled back” value of \(Y\) from a nearby point along the flow of \(X\).

2. As a derivation:

Equivalently, and often more practically useful, the Lie derivative of \(Y\) with respect to \(X\) can be defined as the Lie bracket \([X, Y]\), which is a new vector field defined by its action on smooth functions:

\(\mathcal{L}_X Y = [X, Y]\) \([X, Y](f) = X(Y(f)) - Y(X(f))\) for all \(f \in C^\infty(M)\)

Here, \(X(f)\) and \(Y(f)\) denote the Lie derivatives of the function \(f\) with respect to \(X\) and \(Y\), respectively (which are just directional derivatives).

3. In local coordinates:

In local coordinates, if \(X = \sum_i X^i \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\) and \(Y = \sum_j Y^j \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\), the Lie bracket is given by:

\([X, Y] = \sum_{i,j} \left( X^i \frac{\partial Y^j}{\partial x^i} - Y^i \frac{\partial X^j}{\partial x^i} \right) \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\)

The Lie derivative (or Lie bracket) of vector fields plays a central role in differential geometry, particularly in the study of Lie groups and Lie algebras. It provides a way to measure how vector fields change along the flow of other vector fields and quantifies the non-commutativity of their flows. It is a fundamental operation capturing the interaction between vector fields.

Using local coordinates \((U, \varphi)\) on the manifold \(M\), where \(\varphi: U \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is the chart map, and expressing the vector field \(V\) as:

  • The initial condition specifies that at time \(t=0\), the integral curve passes through the point \(p\), which has coordinate values \(x_0^i\).

\(\Phi^V(t, p) = \varphi^{-1}(x(t))\)

where \(x(t) = (x^1(t), \dots, x^n(t))\) is the solution to the system of ODEs. The initial point is specified by \(\varphi(p)=(x_0^1, \dots, x_0^n)\).

How to do the Taylor expansion of \(\Phi^V(t,p) = \gamma_p(t)\) around \(t=0\)?

To perform the Taylor expansion of \(\Phi^V(t, p) = \gamma_p(t)\) around \(t = 0\), we need to use the fact that \(\gamma_p(t)\) satisfies the system of ODEs defining the integral curves, along with the initial condition \(\gamma_p(0) = p\). Let’s work in local coordinates \((U, \varphi)\) around \(p\), where the coordinates of \(p\) are given by \(x_0 = \varphi(p)\). The ODEs are:

\(\frac{d x^i}{dt}(t) = V^i(x(t))\)

where \(x(t) = \varphi(\gamma_p(t)) = (x^1(t), \dots, x^n(t))\) are the local coordinates of \(\gamma_p(t)\).

Taylor Expansion:

The Taylor expansion of \(x^i(t)\) around \(t = 0\) is given by:

\(x^i(t) = x^i(0) + t \left( \frac{dx^i}{dt}\Big|_{t=0} \right) + \frac{t^2}{2!} \left( \frac{d^2x^i}{dt^2}\Big|_{t=0} \right) + \frac{t^3}{3!} \left( \frac{d^3x^i}{dt^3}\Big|_{t=0} \right) + \dots\)

Now, let’s compute the derivatives:

  • First derivative: \(\frac{dx^i}{dt}\Big|_{t=0} = V^i(x(0)) = V^i(x_0)\) (This follows directly from the ODE).

  • Second derivative: To compute the second derivative, we differentiate the ODE with respect to \(t\):

\(\frac{d^2 x^i}{dt^2} = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{\partial V^i}{\partial x^k}(x(t)) \frac{dx^k}{dt}(t) = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{\partial V^i}{\partial x^k}(x(t)) V^k(x(t))\)

Evaluating at \(t=0\), we get:

\(\frac{d^2 x^i}{dt^2}\Big|_{t=0} = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{\partial V^i}{\partial x^k}(x_0) V^k(x_0) = V(V^i)(x_0)\), which is the \(i\)-th component of \(V(V(x_0))\).

  • Higher-order derivatives: Higher-order derivatives can be computed recursively by repeatedly differentiating the ODE, involving higher-order partial derivatives of the \(V^i\) functions and more applications of the vector field \(V\) as a derivation.

Taylor Expansion up to Second Order:

Combining the derivatives, the Taylor expansion up to second order is:

\(x^i(t) = x_0^i + t V^i(x_0) + \frac{t^2}{2} V(V^i)(x_0) + \mathcal{O}(t^3)\)

This can be written more compactly using the vector notation (recalling that \(x_0 = \varphi(p)\)):

\(x(t) = x_0 + t V(x_0) + \frac{t^2}{2} V(V(x_0)) + \mathcal{O}(t^3)\)

Finally, using the inverse chart map to get back to the manifold M:

\(\Phi^V(t,p) = \varphi^{-1}\left( x_0 + t V(x_0) + \frac{t^2}{2} V(V(x_0)) + \mathcal{O}(t^3) \right)\)

This shows how to obtain the Taylor expansion up to second order. The higher-order terms become increasingly complex but follow a similar pattern involving higher-order derivatives of the component functions of \(V\).

1-form on manifolds

What is a 1-form defined on the manifold \(M\)

A 1-form on a smooth manifold \(M\) is a smooth section of the cotangent bundle \(T^*M\). Let’s break down what this means:

  1. Cotangent Bundle: The cotangent bundle \(T^*M\) is the disjoint union of all cotangent spaces of \(M\): \(T^*M = \bigcup_{p \in M} T_p^*M\). More precisely, it’s the set of all pairs \((p, \omega_p)\), where \(p \in M\) and \(\omega_p \in T_p^*M\) is a covector (one-form) at \(p\). It has a natural projection map \(\pi: T^*M \to M\) such that \(\pi(p, \omega_p) = p\).

  2. Section of a Bundle: A section of a bundle is a map that assigns to each point of the base space (in this case, \(M\)) an element in the fiber above that point. More formally, a 1-form \(\omega\) is a smooth map:

    \(\omega: M \to T^*M\)

    such that \(\pi \circ \omega = \text{id}_M\), where \(\text{id}_M\) is the identity map on \(M\). This condition means that for each \(p \in M\), the covector \(\omega(p)\) is an element of the cotangent space \(T_p^*M\). In other words, \(\omega\) assigns a covector to each point of the manifold \(M\).

  3. Smoothness: The condition that \(\omega\) is a smooth map means that in local coordinates, the components of the 1-form are smooth functions. Given a chart \((U, \varphi)\) on \(M\), we can write \(\omega(p) = \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_i(p) dx^i|_p\), where \(\omega_i(p)\) are real numbers representing the components of \(\omega(p)\) in the coordinate basis \(\{dx^1|_p, \dots, dx^n|_p\}\) and \(\omega_i: U \to \mathbb{R}\) are smooth functions.

In simpler terms:

At each point \(p\) on the manifold, a 1-form \(\omega\) assigns a linear functional \(\omega_p: T_pM \to \mathbb{R}\) to the tangent space at that point. This linear functional takes a tangent vector \(v_p\) as input and gives a real number \(\omega_p(v_p)\) as output. The smoothness condition ensures that this assignment varies smoothly as the point \(p\) moves across the manifold. The one form acts on a vector field to produce a smooth function.

Therefore, a 1-form \(\omega\) on \(M\) is formally defined as a smooth section of the cotangent bundle \(T^*M\). This means it’s a smooth map that assigns a covector \(\omega(p) \in T_p^*M\) to each point \(p \in M\). The smoothness condition ensures that the 1-form varies smoothly across the manifold. One forms are also referred to as differential one forms.

At a point \(p \in M\), using a chart \((U,\varphi)\), how to show that \(\{dx^1 |_p, \cdots, dx^n |_p\}\) is the coordinate basis for \(T^*_pM\)

To show that \(\{dx^1|_p, \dots, dx^n|_p\}\) forms a basis for the cotangent space \(T_p^*M\) at a point \(p\) given a chart \((U, \varphi)\), we need to demonstrate that these one-forms are linearly independent and span \(T_p^*M\). Let’s use the properties of the dual basis.

  1. Coordinate Basis for \(T_pM\): The chart \((U, \varphi)\) provides a coordinate system \((x^1, \dots, x^n)\) on \(U\). The partial derivative operators at \(p\):

    \(\left\{ \frac{\partial}{\partial x^1}\Big|_p, \dots, \frac{\partial}{\partial x^n}\Big|_p \right\}\)

    form a basis for the tangent space \(T_pM\). These are derivations that act on smooth functions \(f \in C^\infty(M)\).

  2. Definition of \(dx^i|_p\): The one-forms \(dx^i|_p\) are defined by their action on the basis vectors of \(T_pM\):

    \(dx^i|_p \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\Big|_p \right) = \delta^i_j\)

    where \(\delta^i_j\) is the Kronecker delta (1 if \(i=j\), 0 otherwise).

  3. Linear Independence: Suppose we have a linear combination of the \(dx^i|_p\) that equals the zero covector:

    \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i dx^i|_p = 0\)

    This means that for every tangent vector \(v_p \in T_pM\), we have \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i dx^i|_p(v_p) = 0\). Applying this equation to the basis vectors \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}|_p\), and using the definition of \(dx^i|_p\), we get:

    \(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i dx^i|_p \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j}\Big|_p \right) = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \delta^i_j = a_j = 0\)

    This is true for all \(j\), proving that the \(dx^i|_p\) are linearly independent.

  4. Spanning \(T_p^*M\): Any covector \(\omega_p \in T_p^*M\) can be expressed as a linear combination of the \(dx^i|_p\):

    \(\omega_p = \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_p \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}\Big|_p \right) dx^i|_p\)

    Let \(a_i = \omega_p \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x^i}|_p \right)\). Then \(\omega_p = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i dx^i|_p\). This shows that the \(dx^i|_p\) span the cotangent space.

Since the one-forms \(\{dx^1|_p, \dots, dx^n|_p\}\) are linearly independent and span \(T_p^*M\), they form a basis for the cotangent space at \(p\), which is called the coordinate basis (also the dual basis) for the cotangent space, given the chart \((U, \varphi)\). The dual basis property is crucial to this proof. The proof relies on selecting basis vectors of \(T_pM\) and demonstrating the dual basis relationship with \(\{dx^1|_p, \dots, dx^n|_p\}\).

Are there other ways to view the 1-form in addition to as a map \(\omega_p: T_pM \to R\)?

Yes, there are several other ways to view a 1-form, depending on the context and what aspects you want to emphasize:

  1. As a Covector: A 1-form \(\omega\) at a point \(p\) assigns a covector \(\omega_p\) to each point \(p\) on the manifold, meaning it is a linear map \(\omega_p: T_p M \to \mathbb{R}\). This is the most fundamental perspective. The linear map takes a tangent vector and returns a real number.

  2. As a Linear Functional on Tangent Vectors: This is closely related to the covector perspective but emphasizes the action of the 1-form on tangent vectors. The 1-form acts as a linear functional, assigning a real number (a scalar) to each tangent vector at a point on the manifold. This number represents the “projection” of the vector onto the 1-form.

  3. Locally as a Differential: In local coordinates \((x^1, \dots, x^n)\) given by a chart \((U, \varphi)\) around a point \(p\), a 1-form \(\omega\) can be written locally as:

    \(\omega = \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_i(x) dx^i\)

    where \(\omega_i(x)\) are smooth functions. In this local representation, the \(dx^i\) are the differentials of the coordinate functions. This viewpoint emphasizes the local coordinate representation and the connection to calculus (the differential).

  4. As a Section of the Cotangent Bundle: As previously discussed, a 1-form is a smooth section of the cotangent bundle \(T^*M\). This perspective emphasizes the global aspect of the 1-form, considering it as a smooth assignment of a covector to each point of the manifold.

  5. As a Linear Map on Vector Fields: A 1-form \(\omega\) acts not just on individual tangent vectors but also on vector fields \(X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\). This action produces a smooth function: \(\omega(X): M \to \mathbb{R}\) which at a point \(p\) is given by \(\omega_p(X_p)\). This viewpoint emphasizes the action of the 1-form as a global linear operator on the module of vector fields. This results in a smooth function on \(M\).

  6. Using the Interior Product: The interior product \(i_X \omega\) of a vector field \(X\) with a 1-form \(\omega\) produces a function on \(M\) whose value at a point \(p\) is \(\omega_p(X_p)\).

The choice of viewpoint often depends on the context. The covector perspective is often most useful for the theoretical development, while the local differential representation is more useful for computational purposes. The linear functional perspective emphasizes the action on vectors while the section of the cotangent bundle emphasizes the global aspect of a 1-form. The linear map on vector fields emphasizes the properties of the one-form acting as a global operator. The interior product representation emphasizes the relationship between the one-form and vector fields.

Geometric Intuition of 1-forms

Provide geometric intuition for a 1-form just like there is one for tangent vectors at a point \(p \in M\).

The geometric intuition for a 1-form at a point \(p \in M\) is less immediate than that for tangent vectors, but we can develop it by considering its action on tangent vectors. While tangent vectors represent directions and magnitudes of movement along the manifold, 1-forms represent a way to measure or assign a value to those directions. Think of it like this:

1. Level Sets: Imagine a smooth function \(f: M \to \mathbb{R}\). The level sets of \(f\) (the sets of points where \(f\) takes on a constant value) form surfaces (or hypersurfaces) in \(M\). The differential \(df_p\) at a point \(p\) is a 1-form. The vector \(df_p\) is normal to the level set at point p. This is only true if the gradient is non zero.

  • Geometrically, \(df_p\) assigns to each tangent vector at \(p\) a measure of how much that vector points in the direction of increasing \(f\). If the vector is tangent to a level set, its value is zero. The value increases as the vector points more in the direction normal to the level set of \(f\) at \(p\).

2. Measuring “Flow”: Consider a 1-form \(\omega\) at \(p\), and imagine a small tangent vector \(v_p\) at \(p\). The value \(\omega_p(v_p)\) can be interpreted as a measure of the “flow” or “flux” through a small surface element oriented in the direction \(v_p\). The 1-form \(\omega\) determines a magnitude (and sign). The magnitude represents the amount of “flow” through the element, and the sign tells us the direction of the flow relative to the 1-form. The magnitude is related to the component of the vector along the 1-form.

3. Dual to Tangent Vectors: The value \(\omega_p(v_p)\) can also be viewed as the “projection” of the tangent vector \(v_p\) onto the 1-form \(\omega_p\), analogous to the dot product in Euclidean space.

4. Linear Functional: Recall that a 1-form \(\omega_p\) is a linear functional on tangent vectors at \(p\). It takes tangent vectors as input and returns a real number, measuring their components along a direction determined by \(\omega_p\). The real number output is a linear function of the input tangent vectors.

In essence, a 1-form at a point can be geometrically viewed as a way to:

  • Measure the component of a tangent vector in a specific direction (related to \(df_p\)).
  • Measure the “flow” or “flux” through an infinitesimal surface element (related to the surface integral).
  • Assign a scalar value to tangent vectors via a linear functional.

These interpretations help visualize 1-forms and understand their role in differential geometry. The intuitive understanding is improved by visualizing the level sets of functions, in the case of the differential of functions. For other 1-forms, the concept of measuring flow is important. The linear functional property is also crucial. The combination of these interpretations can provide a solid intuitive grasp of a one-form.

The interpretation of the 1-form in terms of level sets of a smooth function needs to be broken down further

Let’s break down the interpretation of a 1-form in terms of level sets of a smooth function in more detail. This interpretation is particularly useful for understanding the differential of a function, which is a 1-form.

1. Smooth Function and Level Sets:

Consider a smooth function \(f: M \to \mathbb{R}\) on a manifold \(M\). A level set of \(f\) is a subset of \(M\) where \(f\) takes on a constant value:

\(L_c = \{p \in M \,|\, f(p) = c\}\) for some \(c \in \mathbb{R}\).

If \(df_p \neq 0\), the level sets of \(f\) locally form hypersurfaces in \(M\) near points where \(f\) is not constant.

2. The Differential as a 1-form:

The differential of \(f\) at a point \(p\), denoted \(df_p\), is a 1-form. It’s a linear map:

\(df_p: T_p M \to \mathbb{R}\)

Its action on a tangent vector \(v_p \in T_p M\) is the directional derivative of \(f\) at \(p\) in the direction of \(v_p\):

\(df_p(v_p) = v_p(f) = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{f(\gamma(t)) - f(p)}{t}\),

where \(\gamma(t)\) is a smooth curve with \(\gamma(0) = p\) and \(\gamma'(0) = v_p\).

3. Geometric Interpretation of \(df_p\) with Level Sets:

  • Normal to Level Sets: If \(df_p \neq 0\), then \(df_p\) is normal to the level set \(L_{f(p)}\) at the point \(p\). This is because the directional derivative of \(f\) along any tangent vector to the level set at p must be zero. The gradient is orthogonal to the level surfaces.

  • Measuring Change Along a Direction: For a tangent vector \(v_p\), the value \(df_p(v_p)\) is the rate of change of the function \(f\) along \(v_p\).

    • If \(v_p\) is tangent to the level set at \(p\), then \(df_p(v_p) = 0\), because moving along the level set doesn’t change the value of \(f\).

    • If \(v_p\) points in the direction of increasing \(f\), then \(df_p(v_p) > 0\).

    • If \(v_p\) points in the direction of decreasing \(f\), then \(df_p(v_p) < 0\).

    • The magnitude of \(df_p(v_p)\) is proportional to the component of \(v_p\) in the direction normal to the level set at \(p\). The magnitude is proportional to the cosine of the angle between the tangent vector and the normal.

In summary:

The differential \(df_p\) is a 1-form that measures the rate of change of the function \(f\) at \(p\) along various directions. Its action on tangent vectors is intimately related to the geometry of the level sets of \(f\): it’s normal to the level sets, and its value on a tangent vector reflects how much that vector points in the direction of increasing \(f\). The magnitude of the value is the component of the tangent vector along the normal to the level set at that point. This geometric interpretation is particularly useful for understanding the differential 1-form in this specific case. For other 1-forms, other geometric interpretations are more appropriate.
